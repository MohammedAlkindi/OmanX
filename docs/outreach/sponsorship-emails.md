## 1a. Anthropic — Claude for Startups

**Ask:** admission to the startup program / API credits
**Apply at:** [claude.com/programs/startups](https://claude.com/programs/startups) — verify current terms at [anthropic.com/startup-program-official-terms](https://www.anthropic.com/startup-program-official-terms)

> **Eligibility reality check, read before sending.** The published criteria for the headline tiers ask for institutional equity funding, incorporation within the last four years, and no prior Anthropic startup credits. OmanX is self-funded and unincorporated, so the $25K–$100K tiers are almost certainly out of reach. The program is documented as open to early-stage founders with or without VC backing, with a base tier reported around $1,000 in credits plus priority rate limits — that's the realistic target. The email below asks for that honestly rather than pretending to be a funded startup. If you incorporate or join an accelerator later, reapplying under the larger tier becomes viable.

**Subject:** Claude for Startups — OmanX, compliance guidance for Omani scholars abroad

Hi [Name],

I'm applying to Claude for Startups for OmanX (omanx.org), an AI guidance assistant for Omani students studying in the US, UK, and Australia. It's live, built on the Claude API, and I should say up front that I'm a solo, self-funded, unincorporated developer — I know that puts me outside the funded-startup tier, and I'm asking about the entry tier and priority rate limits rather than the headline package.

The reason I think it's worth your attention is the failure mode the product is designed around. A general assistant will confidently hallucinate an OPT deadline or a SEVIS reinstatement procedure. For a student on an F-1, Student Route, or Subclass 500 visa, that isn't a bad answer — it's a status violation with immigration consequences.

So OmanX constrains the model rather than trusting it. Compliance questions are separated from everyday ones by a deterministic classifier and routed into a curated per-destination knowledge base plus live search restricted to an allowlist of roughly fifteen government domains — `uscis.gov`, `gov.uk`, `homeaffairs.gov.au`. Every compliance answer cites the specific knowledge base entry or source URL it used, and the system prompt instructs the model to say "I don't know, ask your DSO" rather than close a gap. Urgent situations return an escalation card built in code, not generated by the model: correct emergency number for the country, correct form numbers, numbered steps — identical every time, immune to model variance. Compliance responses are never cached, because a stale visa answer is worse than a slow one.

That's a fairly concrete instance of using Claude where being wrong carries legal cost, and of designing the surrounding system so the model can't be the single point of failure. I'd be glad to write it up in more detail if that's useful to the team.

The tool is free to students. Message caps are set by my budget rather than by need — currently [X] anonymous and [Y] daily for signed-in users. Credits would go directly to raising those.

Mohammed Alkindi
omanx.org | [github.com/YOUR-HANDLE]

## 2. MoHE / cultural attachés

**Targets:** Ministry of Higher Education; Omani cultural attachés in Washington, London, Canberra
**Ask:** accuracy review and permission to cite — deliberately *not* money or a pilot

**Subject:** OmanX — guidance tool for Omani scholars abroad, requesting an accuracy review

Dear [Title Name],

I'm Mohammed Alkindi, an Omani [student at X / developer]. I built OmanX (omanx.org), a free assistant that answers questions from Omani scholars in the US, UK, and Australia — campus logistics at one end, and visa status, work authorization, insurance, and academic standing at the other.

I'm writing with a specific and low-cost request, not a funding one.

OmanX maintains a set of Ministry rules — scholarship terms, notification requirements — that is merged into every answer alongside destination-specific immigration guidance. I compiled that content from public sources. I would like it verified by someone at the Ministry, so that scholars are not relying on my reading of the rules.

Concretely, I'm asking for two things:

1. A review of the MoHE content set for accuracy.
2. If it passes review, permission to state that it reflects official guidance.

What I want to be clear about: OmanX is an independent, self-funded tool. It has no institutional dashboard, no data sharing with the Ministry, and no access to scholar records. Every compliance answer cites its source, and anything status-critical instructs the student to confirm with their attaché or their university's international student office.

If a broader institutional pilot becomes of interest later, I would welcome that conversation. But the accuracy review is what matters most right now, and it's the thing I can't do alone.

Respectfully,
Mohammed Alkindi
omanx.org

> **Why no money ask:** a ministry can't act on a funding request from an unknown solo developer, but it can act on "check my facts." That's a near-zero-cost yes, and it converts into the endorsement that makes every other conversation on this list easier.
